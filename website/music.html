Music of the Akan and Mande People

The music of the Akan and Mande people is highly connected to royalty and military. A style of playing called Fontomfrom is very common. It uses multiple different types of drums and is very complex. It uses additive rhythms and each drum line plays an independant rhythm that cycles.

Instruments

The Kora

The Kora is a hollowed out gourd with a spike in the middle, with strings attached to the spike. Every string is tuned differently and is a different legnth so it can play a veriety of pitches. There are different techniques, such as Kumbengo and Brimintango. Kumbengo is a polyphonic style played by using your thumbs to play the bassline, your middle fingers to play the harmony lines, and your outer fingers to play the melody line. Brimintango is played by improvising virtuosic flourishes in the melody line.

The Dawuro

The Dawuro is a high pitched percussion instrument that is commonly found in Fontomfrom ensembles. It has two bells made of metal that are different sizes. The smaller one plays a higher note and the bigger one plays a lower note, separated by a large interval.

The Eguankoba

The Eguankoba is a medium pitched drum played in Fontomfrom. It is played with the hands and makes a medium pitch.

The From

The From is a large drum played in Fontomfrom music. It is played with the hands and produces a low pitch.

Vocal Music

Vocal music in Akan and Mande culture is melismatic. A style of singing known as Satoro is where you sing roughly and emotionally.

Elements of Akan and Mande Music

Akan and Mande music is played in groups of experienced musicians. A caste of musicians, called the Jeli or Griots, are the only people who can play music. They are trained and experienced and highly skilled.
